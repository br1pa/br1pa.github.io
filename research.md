# Research

My research sits at the intersection of Causal AI, Econometrics, Operations Research, and Machine Learning. I am particularly interested in building interpretable and decision-relevant models for complex environments.

My work spans both methodological and applied research, with projects in finance, healthcare, and causal structure learning.

## Research interests

- Causal AI
- Probabilistic Graphical Models
- Operations Research
- Decision Theory
- Econometrics

## Current research

### Time-series causal discovery with variable lags

My current PhD research focuses on causal discovery in multivariate time series with variable delays between causes and effects.

### Market microstructure and simulation

At the Financial Conduct Authority, I have developed a bilateral RFQ-driven simulation of the UK gilt market to study dealer and investor behaviour under market stress. This work models abstract GEMMs, institutional investors, LDI managers, hedge funds, and central-bank intervention scenarios, with a focus on liquidity, spreads, volatility, and market abuse dynamics.

This research combines simulation, market design, behavioural assumptions, and policy-facing analysis to better understand resilience and supervision in fixed-income markets.

### Causal inference and structure learning

My academic research has explored causal discovery, Bayesian networks, and structure learning in complex real-world systems. This includes work on healthcare applications such as sepsis, as well as broader methodological questions around causal structure learning and policy evaluation.

A central theme in this work is the use of interpretable models to move beyond pure prediction and towards explanation, intervention, and decision-making.

### Applied machine learning in healthcare

My earlier work in the NHS focused on predictive-risk modelling, causal analysis, and health-economics applications. This included work on self-harm risk classification, and budget-impact modelling for healthcare interventions.

These projects aimed to connect statistical and machine-learning methods to operational decisions and real-world outcomes in healthcare systems.

## Ongoing themes

Across these projects, I am motivated by a common question: how can we move from prediction to explanation, and from explanation to better decisions in complex systems?

My core research interest is causality. Although I work across statistical modelling, econometrics, Bayesian AI, structure learning, simulation, and machine learning, these methodologies are connected by a shared aim: understanding cause-and-effect relationships in dynamic real-world systems. I am interested not only in whether variables are associated, but in how interventions propagate through a system, how delayed effects emerge over time, and how models can support reasoning about policy, risk, and decision-making.

This interest in causality shapes both my methodological and applied work. Whether I am studying causal structure learning, Bayesian networks, market microstructure, healthcare risk, or supervisory decision-support, I am ultimately trying to build models that are interpretable, decision-relevant, and useful in high-stakes settings.

My research emphasizes:

- interpretability over black-box prediction alone
- causal and probabilistic reasoning
- operational usefulness in complex environments
